The Problem You're Solving
You've registered. You know you're building something around AI. What you may not have is a clear picture of how the four days actually unfold, what the workspace channels are for, what you're allowed to do, and what happens to what you build.

Not knowing the rules costs time. Teams that understand the structure from the start spend those first hours building instead of asking basic questions. This guide covers the essential rules, the workspace setup, and the development stages you'll move through, so nothing surprises you mid-hackathon.

Step 2: Understand Eligibility and Team Rules
Who can compete:

The hackathon is open to participants of any gender, industry, age, and experience level. If you're under 18, you'll need a consent form signed by a guardian before the event. There's no fee to participate.

Team size:

Teams can have 1 to 6 participants. Solo participation is allowed. If you don't have a team, find one through the #find-a-team channel.

Changing your team:

To add a member, re-submit the team registration form with the updated details. To remove a member, submit a new form with the corrected roster. The most recent version is always the one on record. If your team name changes during this process, note that in the form.

Changing your challenge:

If you want to switch challenge categories, you don't need to re-register your team. Update your choice in the final submission form when you submit. Just make sure you select the correct challenge at that point.

# Step 3: Know the Development Stages
Teams move through three phases during the hackathon. Understanding what each phase demands helps you allocate your time during the hackathon build phase more deliberately.
**Idea generation (first several hours):**
This is where you converge on a problem worth solving and a direction worth building. The temptation is to keep exploring options. The discipline is committing to something specific quickly enough to have time to build it. Mentors are especially useful here for feasibility checks and domain perspective.
**Rapid build (the bulk of the hackathon):**
This is where you build the thing. The key is protecting your core demonstration from scope expansion. Build the minimum version that proves the concept before you add features. Done is better than perfect when you have four days.
**Demos and submission (final stretch):**
Shift focus to packaging the work. This includes finishing the video, completing the document, cleaning up the code repository, and submitting everything before the deadline. Allocate more time to this than you think you'll need. Most teams underestimate how long the submission packaging takes.

Step 4: Know What to Submit and When
Submission components:

Your submission should include:

A working prototype or application
A short presentation or demo explaining your solution and its impact
The deadline:

The hackathon closes at 9AM CT on March 9. Submissions are made using the official Team Registration & Submission Form shared in #announcements.

Late submissions:

Late submissions are accepted but penalised. Teams lose 1 point for every 2-hour window past the deadline. Submitting at 9:01AM loses you 1 point. Submitting at 11:01AM loses you 2 points. The maximum penalty is 5 points.

Step 5: Understand Judging and Scoring
All submissions are scored by the GenAI Works judging panel against four criteria totalling 40 points. Organizers screen entries first; shortlisted finalists are passed to judges.

Consistency with challenge statements carries the most weight at 15 points. This measures how directly your solution addresses the problem. Quality and design is 10 points, assessed via your code repository or design artefacts. Originality and impact is 10 points: how different is your solution from what exists, and how meaningful is the potential effect. Commercialisation potential is 5 points: is there a plausible path for this to exist beyond the hackathon?

Bonus points:

Projects that integrate Bright Data (a hackathon sponsor) may receive bonus points, though its use is optional.

Ideas to Get You Started
For first-time hackathon participants: A hackathon is an invention marathon, not a polished product launch. The expectation is a working demonstration of a novel idea, not a market-ready solution. That framing reduces a lot of anxiety about what you're supposed to produce.

For teams deciding between challenge categories: Read each challenge description carefully before committing. The consistency with challenge statements criterion is worth 15 of 40 points. A solution that's technically stronger but less directly relevant to your chosen challenge will score lower than a simpler solution that precisely addresses it.

For teams thinking about code quality: The GitHub repository is how judges assess technical quality. A repository with clear structure, readable code, and basic documentation scores better than one that works but is hard to navigate. Clean it up before you submit.

For teams worried about commercial potential: You don't need a full business plan. You need to have thought about who the customer is, how they'd pay for it (or how it would be funded), and what the scale of the opportunity looks like. One clear paragraph in your document covering this is sufficient.

For teams across time zones: The four-day window from Friday 9pm to Monday 9pm EST was designed to give global teams flexible working time. Use the Friday evening and Monday morning slots intentionally for team members who can't be available on the weekend.
